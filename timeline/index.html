1936
Alan Turing
Turing Machine
Alan Turing defines a mathematical model of computation: an infinite tape, a read/write head, and a transition table. The model proves what can and cannot be computed.
Significance: The theoretical foundation for every digital computer. Every programming language and every processor implements the same computational model.

1950
Alan Turing
Turing Test
Alan Turing publishes a paper asking: "Can machines think?" He proposes a test: if a human judge cannot distinguish between a machine and a person in a text conversation, the machine "thinks."
Significance: Turing defined a criterion for measuring artificial intelligence before the technology even existed.

1989
Yann LeCun
LeNet — Handwritten Digit Recognition
Yann LeCun develops LeNet — a convolutional neural network (CNN) that recognizes handwritten digits. The US Postal Service (USPS) uses it to read zip codes.
Significance: The first practical use of a neural network in production. Proof that networks can solve real-world problems, not just academic ones.
CNN Visualization →
2001
Jimmy Wales Larry Sanger
Wikipedia — The Free Encyclopedia
Wikipedia launches as a free online encyclopedia that anyone can edit. Within a few years it accumulated millions of articles in hundreds of languages — the largest open knowledge repository in the world.
Significance for AI: Wikipedia became the primary data source for training language models. Nearly every modern NLP model was trained on its texts. Its structured knowledge (categories, links, infoboxes) feeds knowledge graphs and search engines.
2004
Google
Google Books
Google launches Google Books — a project to scan and digitize millions of books from libraries around the world. To date, over 40 million volumes have been scanned.
Significance for AI: The project created a massive text corpus that led to tools like Google Ngram Viewer for analyzing language trends over hundreds of years. The digitized books were used to train language models and develop advanced OCR.
2009–2012
Geoffrey Hinton Yann LeCun Yoshua Bengio Alex Krizhevsky
Deep Networks + GPU
Neural networks — the idea has existed since the 1950s, but computers were too weak. Hinton, LeCun, and Bengio prove that deep networks can learn complex patterns.

GPUs — graphics cards built for gaming perform billions of matrix multiplications in parallel.
2012: AlexNet uses a GPU and wins the image recognition competition by a large margin. The GPU became the foundational hardware of AI.
2013
Tomas Mikolov
Word Vectors — Word2Vec
A neural network that maps words to vectors in a high-dimensional space. Similar words get nearby vectors. Vector arithmetic reflects semantic relationships:

king − man + woman = queen
Significance: Word relationships are learned from text, not from hand-written rules.

2017
Ashish Vaswani Noam Shazeer Niki Parmar Jakob Uszkoreit
Transformer Architecture
The paper "Attention Is All You Need" introduces the Transformer — a structure that processes entire sentences in parallel using an Attention mechanism, instead of word-by-word.
Significance: Parallel processing fully utilizes the GPU. All modern language models use components from this architecture.
Transformer Visualization →
2022
OpenAI Sam Altman
ChatGPT — AI for Everyone
OpenAI launches ChatGPT in November 2022 — a simple chat interface on top of the GPT-3.5 model. Within five days — one million users. Within two months — 100 million. The fastest-growing product in history.
Significance: For the first time, anyone can chat with a language model without technical knowledge. ChatGPT transformed AI from an academic topic into an everyday tool, and launched an arms race among the major tech companies.

2023
Hugo Touvron
Open Source — Llama
Until 2023, the most powerful language models were closed-source. Llama was released as open source.
Significance: Relatively small models on affordable hardware achieve performance close to closed-source models. Accessible to researchers and startups.
2024–2026
Tri Dao Benjamin Lee
Long Context Window
The context window = the amount of text the model processes at once. Previously: a few pages. Text beyond the window — the model doesn't see it.

Gemini and others expanded the window to millions of tokens.
Significance: An entire book, a codebase, or an hour of video — in a single request.
